Introduction

AI will accelerate biological research by roughly 2.8x by 2030‚Äîcompressing what would have been a century of progress into approximately 35 years. That's 64 years of human suffering prevented: diseases that would have killed people, disabilities that would have limited lives, mysteries that would have remained unsolved.

But 2.8x is far less than many expect. If AlphaFold predicts protein structures 24x faster than experiments, why isn't structural biology accelerating 24x? The answer reveals something fundamental about the nature of scientific progress.

I call this the pipeline problem: the gap between how fast AI can perform individual computational tasks and how fast complete research programs actually move.

AlphaFold revolutionizes the structure prediction step‚Äîtaking it from weeks to minutes. But the physical steps before and after? Largely unchanged. You still need to express the protein. You still need cryo-EM to validate predictions. You still need wet lab experiments.

Key Concept

The pipeline discount factor measures how much of an AI tool's raw speedup actually translates into faster research outcomes. For structural biology, AlphaFold's 24x speedup becomes ~9x pipeline acceleration (discount factor: 0.37).

The Materials Science Paradox

Look at that materials science row. GNoME, DeepMind's materials discovery system, can predict stable crystal structures 2000x faster than traditional methods. It identified 2.2 million potentially stable materials in a single study.

Yet the pipeline discount is 0.0007. Nearly zero. Why?

The Materials Science Paradox

GNoME predicted 2.2M materials in hours. At current synthesis rates (~1,000/year), it would take 2,200 years to make them all.

Why Drug Discovery Is Constrained

Drug discovery presents the starkest illustration of the pipeline problem. Clinical trials‚Äîtesting drugs in actual humans‚Äîtake up 60-70% of development time. And you cannot speed up biology.

Drug Development Timeline
Key Insight: AI helps with the first 25% of drug development. It barely touches the last 75%. Regulatory innovation may ultimately matter more than AI innovation for drug development timelines.

The Three Types of Science Work

Understanding why bottlenecks exist requires categorizing research activities:

üñ•Ô∏è Bits-Only Tasks (AI Excels)

Activities that happen entirely in silico‚Äîprediction, simulation, analysis. AI can provide 10x, 100x, even 1000x speedups.

üî¨ Hybrid Tasks (AI Helps)

Physical experiments guided by computation. AI can make these 2-5x faster by making smarter choices, but you still need to do the experiment.

‚öõÔ∏è Atoms-Bound Tasks (AI Can't Help)

Activities determined by physical, biological, or social processes. Clinical trials, chemical synthesis, regulatory review. Minimal acceleration regardless of AI capability.

The Core Insight

Improving AI further will have diminishing returns in domains where Atoms-Bound activities dominate. The bottleneck has shifted from computation to physical reality.

What Would Change Everything

If we want to accelerate beyond 2.8x, we need to address the physical bottlenecks:

  1. Autonomous Synthesis Facilities ($1-2B globally) ‚Äî Robot labs running 24/7 could increase materials throughput 10-50x
  2. Expanded Cryo-EM Infrastructure ($200-500M) ‚Äî Let structural biology validation keep pace with predictions
  3. Clinical Trial Reform ‚Äî Adaptive designs, surrogate endpoints, and international harmonization could cut timelines 30-50%

Conclusion

The pipeline problem is real, but it's not a reason for pessimism. It's a diagnosis that enables targeted treatment.

AI has given us extraordinary capabilities in prediction, analysis, and design. But to capture the full potential, we need to address the physical bottlenecks that currently constrain translation from computational insight to practical impact.

A 2.8x acceleration is already worth celebrating‚Äîit represents tens of millions of lives improved through faster medical progress. But we shouldn't accept it as the ceiling. The pipeline problem tells us exactly where to push for more.
