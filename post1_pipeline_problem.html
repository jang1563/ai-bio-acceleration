The Compressed Century

AI will accelerate biological research by roughly 2.8x by 2030‚Äîcompressing what would have been a century of progress into approximately 35 years. That's 64 years of human suffering prevented: diseases that would have killed people, disabilities that would have limited lives, mysteries that would have remained unsolved.

But 2.8x is far less than many expect. If AlphaFold predicts protein structures 24x faster than experiments, why isn't structural biology accelerating 24x? The answer reveals something fundamental about the nature of scientific progress‚Äîand points to exactly where we need to push to unlock more.

I call this the pipeline problem: the gap between how fast AI can perform individual computational tasks and how fast complete research programs actually move.

Why Bottlenecks Exist

The pipeline discount isn't a temporary limitation that better AI will overcome. It reflects something fundamental about research: different stages have different susceptibility to computational acceleration.

The Bits, Hybrid, and Atoms Framework

Bits-Only Tasks (Pure Computation) ‚Äî Activities that happen entirely in silico: prediction, simulation, analysis. AI excels here, providing 10x, 100x, even 1000x speedups.

Hybrid Tasks (Computationally-Guided Physical Work) ‚Äî Physical experiments guided by computation. AI can make these 2-5x faster by making smarter choices, but you still need to do the experiment.

Atoms-Bound Tasks (Irreducibly Physical) ‚Äî Activities determined by physical, biological, or social processes that computation cannot alter. Clinical trials, chemical synthesis, regulatory review. Minimal acceleration regardless of AI capability.

The Core Insight

Improving AI further will have diminishing returns in domains where Atoms-Bound activities dominate. The bottleneck has shifted from computation to physical reality.

The Materials Science Paradox

Look at that materials science row. GNoME, DeepMind's materials discovery system, can predict stable crystal structures 2000x faster than traditional methods. It identified 2.2 million potentially stable materials‚Äîmore than humanity had characterized in all of history.

Yet the pipeline discount is 0.0007. Nearly zero. Why?

Because actually synthesizing those materials is painfully slow. The A-Lab at Lawrence Berkeley can synthesize ~1,000 new materials per year. At that rate, working through GNoME's predictions would take over 2,000 years.

The Ultimate Pipeline Discount: 2000x computational speedup becomes 1.3x real-world speedup. The discount factor is 0.0007‚Äîmeaning 99.93% of the AI's speed advantage gets absorbed by physical reality.

Why Drug Discovery Is Constrained

Drug discovery presents the starkest illustration of the pipeline problem. Clinical trials‚Äîtesting drugs in actual humans‚Äîtake up 60-70% of development time. And you cannot speed up biology.

If a drug prevents heart attacks, you need to wait years to see if fewer people have heart attacks. There's no shortcut. AI helps with the first 25% of drug development. It barely touches the last 75%.

The Path Forward

The pipeline problem is real, but it's not a reason for pessimism. It's a diagnosis that enables targeted treatment.

AI has given us extraordinary capabilities in prediction, analysis, and design. But to capture the full potential, we need to address the physical bottlenecks‚Äîsynthesis capacity, validation infrastructure, clinical trial efficiency‚Äîthat currently constrain translation from computational insight to practical impact.

A 2.8x acceleration is already worth celebrating‚Äîit represents tens of millions of lives improved through faster medical progress. But we shouldn't accept it as the ceiling. The pipeline problem tells us exactly where to push for more.
